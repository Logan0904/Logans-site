Most people shopping for a portable power station start by comparing prices, then get confused when a $200 unit and a $900 unit both claim to "power your home in an outage." The number that actually separates them, watt-hours, rarely gets explained in plain terms on the product page. So people either overbuy and pay for capacity they'll never use, or underbuy and find out mid-outage that their new power station can't run the one thing they bought it for.

The fix is simpler than it looks once you separate two numbers that get used interchangeably but mean different things.

Watts measure how much power something draws right now, at this instant. Watt-hours measure how much total energy is stored in the battery, which determines how long it can supply that power. A power station rated for 600 watts can run a 600-watt device, but only for however long its watt-hour capacity allows. Mixing these two up is the single most common reason people end up with the wrong unit.

## Figuring out what your devices actually draw

Every device has a running wattage, and some have a much higher startup wattage for the first second or two, which matters for things with a compressor or motor. A phone typically draws 15 to 30 watts while charging. A laptop is usually 45 to 65 watts. A Wi-Fi router and modem together run around 20 to 60 watts, low enough that keeping the internet up during an outage is one of the cheapest things to protect. A CPAP without a heated humidifier draws roughly 30 to 60 watts, though heated tubing pushes that higher. Mini fridges land around 100 watts on average, and a full-size fridge compressor cycles between roughly 100 and 400 watts depending on the model and how often the door opens.

Once you know the running wattage, the runtime math is straightforward: take the power station's usable watt-hours, multiply by about 0.85 to account for inverter losses, and divide by the device's wattage. A 500Wh station running a 60-watt CPAP works out to roughly seven hours, for example. That 0.85 factor isn't marketing fine print, it's just the energy lost converting the battery's stored DC power into the AC power most devices actually need.

## Matching capacity to your situation

Under 300Wh is enough for phones, tablets, a few lights, and short laptop sessions, basically a phone-charging emergency kit rather than real backup power. It won't get most people through an overnight outage if anything more demanding is involved.

The 300 to 500Wh range is where things start to feel like real backup. This tier comfortably covers a laptop, a router and modem, lighting, and a CPAP for a single night, which covers a surprising number of actual outage scenarios for one or two people.

Step up to 500 to 1000Wh and you can run a CPAP across multiple nights, keep a mini fridge going for several hours at a stretch, and still have headroom for phones and a laptop on top of that. From 1000 to 2000Wh, a mini fridge can run essentially all day, and you get what most people would call genuine moderate home backup, covering several devices simultaneously without constantly doing math about what you can and can't turn on at once. If you're specifically trying to keep a full-size fridge alive, that's the tier where it becomes realistic rather than a stretch.

## Don't forget to stack your devices, not just pick the biggest one

The math above is per device, but outages rarely involve running just one thing. If your real scenario is a router, a laptop, and a CPAP all running at the same time overnight, add the wattages together before you divide into the battery's watt-hours. A 500Wh station splitting its capacity across three devices at once drains a lot faster than the same station running just the CPAP alone. This is where a lot of people get surprised: the spec sheet said "runs a CPAP for 8 hours," and that's true in isolation, but nobody mentioned that plugging in a laptop at the same time cuts that runtime by a third or more.

It also helps to think about which devices actually need to run continuously versus which ones just need periodic top-offs. A router benefits from staying on the whole time so you don't lose internet, but a laptop or phone just needs an hour or two of charging here and there. Prioritizing the continuous loads when you size your capacity, rather than assuming everything runs nonstop, usually means you can get away with a smaller and cheaper unit than you'd otherwise think you need.

## Why the spec sheet number and the real number rarely match

Manufacturers test capacity under close to ideal conditions, room temperature, a battery that isn't degraded yet, and a steady load rather than the on-and-off cycling most devices actually do. In practice you'll usually see 10 to 20 percent less usable runtime than the rated watt-hours suggest, on top of the inverter loss already baked into that 0.85 multiplier. Cold weather makes this worse, since lithium batteries lose some effective capacity below freezing, which matters if you're storing the unit in an unheated closet or a car during a winter outage. None of this means the spec sheet is lying, it just means padding your estimate by rounding up to the next capacity tier is a better habit than assuming the number on the box is exactly what you'll get in a real outage at 9pm in January.

## A quick way to size your own setup

List every device you'd actually want powered during a realistic outage, not a worst-case fantasy where you're also running a space heater and a hair dryer. Add up the running wattages for whatever runs simultaneously, note how many hours you want that to last, and multiply the two together. That number, divided by 0.85, is roughly the usable watt-hour capacity you're shopping for. Round up to the next common capacity tier, since real-world runtime always comes in a bit under the spec sheet once temperature and battery age are factored in.

If your list is short, mostly phones, a router, and maybe a CPAP, you're probably fine in the 300 to 500Wh range and can save money by not overbuying. If it includes a fridge or you're regularly dealing with outages longer than eight hours, budget for 1000Wh or more from the start rather than buying twice.
