If you've searched for backup power options, you've probably noticed that most guides assume you own a house and can bolt a battery to the wall. That's not useful if you rent, live in an apartment, or just want something you can move between a closet and a car trunk. This guide is about the category that actually fits that situation: portable power stations.

## What a portable power station actually is

A portable power station is a large rechargeable battery pack with built-in AC outlets, USB ports, and sometimes a car-style 12V output, housed in a case with a handle. Think of it as a much bigger, much more capable version of the battery pack you'd charge a phone with — sized to run a mini fridge, a CPAP machine, a laptop and modem during an outage, or power tools at a job site with no outlet nearby.

They differ from a "generator" in one key way: no fuel, no exhaust, no noise. They differ from a fixed home battery system in that you can unplug them and carry them somewhere else, and installation is "plug it in," not an electrician's visit.

## The three numbers that actually matter

Every listing throws a wall of specs at you. Three of them determine whether a unit will actually do what you need:

**Capacity (watt-hours, Wh).** This is how much total energy the battery holds. A phone charge uses roughly 15-20 Wh, a laptop charge 50-70 Wh, a mini fridge running for a day can use 500-1,500 Wh depending on efficiency and ambient temperature. If you want to run something for hours rather than just top off devices, capacity is the number to compare first — and it's also the number that drives price the most directly.

**Continuous output (watts, W).** This is how much power the unit can deliver at once, not how long it lasts. A unit can have huge capacity and still be unable to start a device with a demanding motor (fridges and power tools often spike well above their "running" wattage on startup). Check a device's startup wattage, not just its running wattage, against the power station's continuous and surge output before assuming it'll work.

**Recharge time and methods.** Some units recharge fully from a wall outlet in under two hours; others take most of a day. If you're buying this specifically for outage prep, a fast AC recharge matters more than solar input specs that most apartment dwellers will never use. If you do want solar charging as a future option, confirm the unit supports it and roughly what wattage of panel it can accept — but treat this as a nice-to-have, not the deciding factor, unless you actually have consistent outdoor access to place panels.

## Picking a capacity tier for your actual situation

Rather than comparing individual models, it's more useful to think in tiers:

**Under 300 Wh** — enough for charging phones, a laptop, small lights, and running a router/modem for many hours during a short outage. Small and light enough to keep in a closet or bag. This tier is a reasonable starting point if your main goal is "keep phones charged and internet up" rather than running appliances.

**300-1,000 Wh** — the middle ground most renters land on. Enough to run a mini fridge for part of a day, keep a CPAP machine running overnight, or power a laptop and lighting through a multi-hour outage. Heavier (often 15-25 lbs) but still movable by one person.

**1,000+ Wh** — approaching small home-backup territory. Can run a full-size fridge for stretches, power multiple devices simultaneously, or serve as a base for longer outages. Heavier and pricier, and worth it mainly if you have a specific medical-device or full-fridge requirement, not just "more is better."

## What to check before buying, specifically

- **Pure sine wave output.** This matters for sensitive electronics (some laptop chargers, medical equipment, audio gear). Cheaper units sometimes output modified sine wave, which can cause issues or reduce efficiency on certain devices. Confirm this in the listing, don't assume it.
- **Pass-through charging support.** Some units let you charge the station and power devices from it at the same time; others don't, or only in specific modes. If you plan to leave it plugged in as a UPS-style backup, confirm this explicitly.
- **Weight and physical size relative to where you'll actually store it.** A unit that's technically "portable" at 30+ lbs is a very different daily experience than one at 10 lbs, especially if you're carrying it up stairs.
- **Warranty length and what it covers.** Battery degradation over charge cycles is normal; a longer warranty (2+ years) is a reasonable signal of build confidence.
