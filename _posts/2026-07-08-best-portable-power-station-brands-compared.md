Once you know what capacity tier you need (our [buying guide](/Logans-site/2026/07/08/portable-power-station-buying-guide-for-renters/) covers that part), the next question is which brand to actually buy. There are dozens of smaller names out there, but four brands dominate this space, and they're genuinely different from each other in ways that matter more than most comparison charts let on.

## Jackery: the easiest one to just hand someone and walk away

Jackery built its reputation on being the simplest power station to actually use. Straightforward display panels, color-coded cables, no learning curve. If you're buying this for a household member who doesn't want to think about wattage math, Jackery is usually the least frustrating option to set up and hand over.

The tradeoff is battery chemistry. A lot of Jackery's lineup still uses NMC lithium cells rated for roughly 800-1,000 charge cycles, noticeably fewer than the LiFePO4 cells most competitors have shifted to. That matters more if you're using the unit frequently (say, as a nightly CPAP backup) than if it's sitting in a closet for occasional outages. Jackery also tends to run pricier than Bluetti or Anker for comparable capacity, and it's generally on the heavier side for its capacity class too.

## EcoFlow: the one that wins on raw output-per-dollar

EcoFlow has carved out a reputation for delivering strong continuous output relative to price, and its LiFePO4-based units are rated for 3,000-plus charge cycles, which matters if you'll actually be cycling the battery regularly rather than leaving it charged in storage. The app and companion features are genuinely well done too, useful if you like monitoring usage remotely rather than just checking a panel.

Where it loses a little ground: some reviewers note the physical build feels less premium than Jackery or Goal Zero, though this rarely affects actual function. For most buyers optimizing for capacity and output per dollar rather than looking for the nicest hardware, this is a reasonable tradeoff.

## Anker (SOLIX): the newer challenger that's caught up fast

Anker's SOLIX line is the newest serious entrant among these four, and it's closed the gap quickly. Like EcoFlow, it uses LiFePO4 chemistry with 3,000-plus rated cycles, and its interface design borrows a lot from Anker's long history in consumer electronics, so it tends to feel polished. If you already own Anker chargers or battery packs, there's a decent chance their app ecosystem already feels familiar.

Because it's newer to this specific category, Anker has less of a long-term track record than Jackery or Goal Zero, though nothing in recent testing suggests that's a real concern. If price-per-watt-hour and modern battery chemistry matter more to you than brand history, it's a reasonable pick.

## Bluetti: the one to check if you might want to scale up later

Bluetti's angle is expandability. A lot of its lineup supports adding extra battery modules later, so you can start with a smaller unit and grow capacity over time instead of buying your final size upfront. That's genuinely useful if you're not sure yet whether you'll want 500Wh or 2,000Wh a year from now, less useful if you already know exactly what you need and don't want to pay for flexibility you won't use.

## Goal Zero: still around, less visible lately

Goal Zero built an early reputation on rugged build quality and a strong solar ecosystem, and some of that reputation still holds. But it shows up far less often in current buying guides and comparisons than the four brands above, which suggests less current market momentum, not necessarily worse products. If you're specifically building a serious solar charging setup and already like Goal Zero's ecosystem, it's still worth a look. For most renters just wanting reliable backup power, one of the other four brands is the more common and better-supported choice right now.

## So which one should you actually buy

If ease of use matters more than saving money, Jackery. If you want the strongest output-per-dollar and don't mind a slightly less premium feel, EcoFlow. If you want modern battery chemistry with a polished interface and don't need years of track record to feel comfortable, Anker. If you might want to expand capacity later instead of committing to one size now, Bluetti. None of these are a wrong answer. They're optimized for slightly different priorities, and the "best" one really does depend on which of those priorities matters to you.
