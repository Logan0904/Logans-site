A chest freezer full of meat or a season's worth of garden produce is exactly the kind of thing that turns a two-day power outage into a real financial loss, not just an inconvenience. Unlike a mini fridge, which most people can live without for a day, a full chest freezer represents actual money sitting in a box. If you're renting and don't have a generator hookup or don't want to deal with fuel storage, a portable power station is the more realistic backup option. Here's how to size one so it doesn't quit on you halfway through the outage.

## What a chest freezer actually pulls

Chest freezers are more power-efficient than upright models because cold air sinks and stays put when you open the lid, so the compressor doesn't have to work as hard to recover. Running watts for a typical chest freezer land somewhere between 100 and 350 watts, with small 5 to 10 cubic foot units on the low end and larger 20 to 25 cubic foot models pulling closer to the top of that range once the compressor kicks in.

Starting watts are the number people miss. When the compressor cycles on, it briefly draws two to three times its running wattage, sometimes spiking as high as 900 watts even on a freezer that only runs at 300 watts steady-state. Your power station's continuous output rating doesn't matter much here. What matters is its surge or peak output rating, which needs to clear that startup spike or the unit just won't turn on. Most power stations in the 1,000 watt-plus continuous range handle this fine, but it's worth checking the spec sheet rather than assuming.

## The part that actually saves you: duty cycle

Here's the detail that makes chest freezers much easier to back up than people expect. A freezer's compressor doesn't run continuously. It cycles on to bring the temperature down, then shuts off and coasts while the insulation holds the cold, then kicks back on once things drift up a few degrees. Realistic duty cycles run 30 to 50 percent, meaning the compressor is actually drawing power less than half the time.

That changes the math a lot. A freezer rated at 200 running watts isn't pulling 200 watts for 24 straight hours, it's closer to 200 watts for 10 to 12 hours spread across the day. Do the math on nameplate wattage alone and you'll think you need double the battery capacity you actually do.

## Sizing examples

Take a mid-size chest freezer pulling 200 running watts at a 40 percent duty cycle. That works out to roughly 80 watts average draw, or around 1,920 watt-hours over a full 24-hour day. A power station in the 1,000Wh class, after accounting for inverter losses (figure on losing 10 to 15 percent of rated capacity to conversion), gets you through roughly 10 to 12 hours before you're recharging. Step up to a 2,000Wh-class unit and you're realistically covering a full day, maybe more if the freezer isn't opened and the ambient temperature stays reasonable.

A smaller chest freezer, 5 to 7 cubic feet, drawing closer to 120 running watts at that same duty cycle, stretches further. Figure on 15 to 18 hours from a 1,000Wh unit, which for a lot of short outages is more than enough.

One thing worth keeping in mind: a freezer that's already full runs more efficiently than a half-empty one, since frozen food holds cold better than air does. If you're prepping for outage season and have room, keeping the freezer fuller than you strictly need to buys you extra runtime margin for free.

## Where things go wrong

Ambient temperature is the variable people forget. A chest freezer sitting in an uninsulated garage in July works a lot harder, and cycles a lot more often, than the same unit in a climate-controlled apartment. If your freezer lives somewhere hot, budget extra capacity or plan on shorter coverage than the numbers above suggest.

Opening the lid resets the clock. Every time you open a chest freezer during an outage, warm air drops in, cold air stays (that's the whole advantage of a chest design over an upright), but the compressor still has to work to recover those few degrees. Minimize how often you open it during an outage and you'll stretch your runtime noticeably.

Modified sine wave inverters aren't automatically dangerous for a freezer compressor the way they can be for medical equipment, but they're not ideal either. Compressor motors on modified sine wave power can run hotter and less efficiently than on pure sine wave, which shortens your runtime and, over repeated use, isn't great for the motor long-term. Most current power stations ship with pure sine wave output anyway, but it's worth confirming on the listing rather than assuming.

## Power station versus generator for this specific job

We've written before about [power stations versus gas generators for apartment living](/Logans-site/2026/07/13/portable-power-station-vs-gas-generator-apartment/), and a chest freezer is a good example of where the calculus tips toward a power station even for renters who technically could store a small generator. Generators need fuel you have to store and rotate, ventilation you may not have (garages count, but attached ones are risky), and they're loud enough that some HOAs and apartment complexes restrict them outright. A power station just sits there charged and ready, silent, and works indoors without a second thought. The tradeoff is capacity: if you need to run a chest freezer for four or five days straight with no recharging option, a generator with a fuel supply wins on raw endurance. For most outages, which resolve in under 48 hours, a properly sized power station covers it without the fuel logistics.

## Recharging mid-outage

If the outage runs longer than your power station's capacity covers, you have three real options: a car with a DC-to-AC inverter or a 12V charging cable (slow, but works in a pinch), a portable solar panel if the unit supports solar input and you have decent weather, or accepting that the freezer's contents may not make it and planning your food storage accordingly. None of these is a great answer, honestly, which is part of why sizing generously upfront matters more for a freezer than for something like phone charging where a partial recharge is no big deal.

## What to look for when buying

For a chest freezer specifically, prioritize a 1,000 to 2,000Wh capacity range depending on freezer size, a continuous output rating comfortably above your freezer's running watts (300W continuous is plenty for most single chest freezers), and a peak or surge rating that clears the startup spike with room to spare. Solar input capability is a nice-to-have if outages in your area tend to run multi-day. LiFePO4 battery chemistry is worth looking for too, since it holds up better to repeated charge cycles than older lithium-ion if you're using this unit for more than just freezer backup.
