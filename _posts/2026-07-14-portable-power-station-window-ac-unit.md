A heat wave knocking out power for a few hours is a different problem than a winter outage. Nobody dies of a cold living room, but a window AC going dark during a July heat advisory is genuinely dangerous for older residents, small kids, or anyone with a health condition that makes heat risky. If you rent and don't have a whole-home generator (or a landlord who'd ever install one), a portable power station is the realistic option for keeping a window unit running through a short outage. The question is whether it's actually big enough to do it, and that depends on math most product pages don't spell out.

## The number that trips people up: starting watts, not running watts

Window AC units list a running wattage on the spec plate, but that's not the number that determines whether your power station can start the unit. Air conditioner compressors pull a big surge of current for the first second or two when the motor kicks on, sometimes three to four times the running draw. A power station that easily handles the running load can still shut down or trip a fault if its surge output can't cover that initial spike.

A 5,000 BTU window unit typically runs around 450 to 500 watts once it's going, but starting watts can hit somewhere in the 1,000 to 1,500 watt range. An 8,000 BTU unit runs closer to 700 to 800 watts, with a startup surge that can climb past 2,500 watts on some models. This is why a power station rated "1000W continuous" on the box doesn't automatically mean it'll run an 8,000 BTU AC, if its surge rating tops out at 1,500W, the compressor never gets past its first second of life.

Before you buy anything, find the actual surge/peak output spec on the power station, not just the continuous watt number, and compare it against your AC's startup draw. Most manufacturers list both, but they're often buried a page deeper than the headline number.

## Sizing by BTU

For a small 5,000 BTU unit, cooling a single bedroom, look for a power station with at least 1,000W continuous output and 2,000W surge capacity. That gives you headroom for the compressor kick without cutting it close. On the low end of watt-hours, 500Wh gets you maybe an hour of runtime accounting for cycling on and off, which is fine for riding out a short outage but not an overnight one.

An 8,000 to 10,000 BTU unit, common in larger bedrooms or small studio apartments, needs a power station rated for at least 1,500 to 2,000W continuous with a surge rating around 3,000 to 4,000W. At that size you're also looking at units in the 1,000 to 2,000Wh capacity range if you want more than a couple hours of actual cooling, since the AC isn't running constantly, it cycles on and off as the thermostat calls for it, so real-world runtime stretches further than dividing wattage into watt-hours would suggest.

Larger 12,000+ BTU units start pushing into territory where a portable power station gets expensive relative to just riding out the outage with fans and closed blinds, though it's still doable if you already own a big-capacity unit for other reasons.

## Cycling changes the math in your favor

Unlike a fridge or CPAP that draws roughly the same wattage the whole time it's on, a window AC's compressor cuts out once the room hits your set temperature and only kicks back on when it drifts back up. That means the average power draw over an hour is meaningfully lower than the running wattage on the spec plate, often 40 to 60 percent of it, depending on room size, insulation, and how hot it is outside. Our [watt-hour sizing guide](/Logans-site/2026/07/13/portable-power-station-watt-hour-sizing-guide/) covers this kind of duty-cycle math in more detail if you're trying to size for other cycling appliances too, like fridges.

Practically, this means a power station that can just barely handle the running wattage will often stretch further than a naive calculation implies, but it does nothing for the surge problem. The compressor still needs that full startup jolt every time it cycles back on, whether that's the first start of the day or the fifteenth.

## What this actually looks like during a heat outage

Realistically, most apartment dwellers aren't trying to run a window AC for eight straight hours off a battery, that's a lot to ask of any portable unit short of the largest and most expensive ones on the market. The more common use case is bridging a gap: keep the room cool enough for two or three hours while waiting out a grid issue, or running the AC in short bursts overnight to knock the temperature down before switching it off and relying on a fan. For that kind of intermittent use, a mid-size power station in the 1,000 to 1,500Wh range with strong surge output does the job without needing to be sized for continuous eight-hour runtime.
