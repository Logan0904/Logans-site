Losing heat in a power outage is a different kind of stressful than losing the fridge or the wifi. Food waits. A cold apartment in January doesn't. So it's a fair question: can a portable power station actually run a space heater, or is this one appliance where the math just doesn't work in an apartment-sized setup?

Short answer: it depends entirely on how long you need heat and how big a station you're willing to buy. Space heaters are power-hungry in a way that catches people off guard, and a unit that easily runs a laptop, a router, and a mini fridge for a day can burn through its whole charge on a space heater in an hour or two. That's not a reason to skip this option. It's a reason to size it correctly before you're standing in a cold apartment during an outage trying to do the math for the first time.

## Why space heaters are the hardest common appliance to back up

Most electric space heaters sold for home use draw 1,500 watts on their high setting, which is the ceiling that a standard 15-amp household circuit is rated to handle safely. Smaller personal heaters run closer to 750 to 1,000 watts, but the 1,500W model is what most people picture when they think "space heater," and it's what tends to actually heat a room rather than just the air near your feet.

Compare that to the other appliances covered on this site. A mini fridge draws maybe 60 to 100 watts running, a CPAP machine 30 to 60 watts, a wifi router under 20 watts. A 1,500W heater is drawing as much as all of those combined many times over. It's simply a much heavier load, and no amount of clever sizing changes that basic fact.

## Running the actual numbers

Here's where it gets uncomfortable. A 1,000Wh power station, a size that handles a fridge or CPAP for most of a day, gives you roughly 35 to 40 minutes of runtime on a 1,500W heater once you account for inverter losses (figure on losing 10 to 15 percent of rated capacity to AC conversion). A 2,000Wh unit gets you to somewhere around an hour and 15 minutes. Even a large 3,000Wh station tops out around two hours of continuous high-setting heat.

None of that is enough to get through an overnight outage on full blast, and it's worth being honest about that upfront rather than discovering it mid-outage. Where this setup actually works is intermittent use: running the heater in bursts to take the edge off a room rather than heating it continuously, or using a lower 750W setting that roughly doubles your runtime compared to the 1,500W setting. A heater with a thermostat that cycles on and off to hold a target temperature will also stretch your runtime further than one running flat out the whole time, since it's only drawing power when the room actually needs it.

## What size station makes sense for this

If space heater backup is your main goal rather than a bonus use case, don't shop in the same 500 to 1,000Wh range that works fine for phones, a fridge, and a CPAP. You're looking at 2,000Wh as a realistic floor, and honestly a 3,000Wh-plus unit if you want more than an hour or two of runtime. That's a bigger investment, both in dollars and in the space it takes up in a closet, so it's worth being clear-eyed about whether you'd rather put that money toward a heater backup plan or a different strategy entirely, like layering blankets, sealing drafts, and closing off unused rooms to reduce how much heat you actually need to generate.

A ceramic or ceramic-fan heater in the 750-1,000W range is a reasonable middle ground if a power station is going to be your primary heat source during outages. You give up some raw heating power compared to a full 1,500W unit, but you roughly double your runtime, which matters more than peak output when you're trying to stretch a battery through several hours.

## Don't overlook the surge

Space heaters themselves don't have much of a startup surge, since they're mostly just a resistive heating element rather than a motor, so this is one appliance where the running wattage and the surge wattage are close together. That's actually good news: you don't need to size around a hidden spike the way you would for a fridge compressor or a sump pump motor. What you do need to check is whether the power station's continuous output rating comfortably clears 1,500W if you're running a full-size heater, since some smaller stations cap continuous output well below that even if their battery capacity looks large enough on paper.

If you're also running a mini fridge, a phone charger, or a wifi router off the same station during an extended outage, add that load to your planning rather than assuming the heater is the only draw. ## What to look for when buying

For space heater backup specifically, prioritize a station with at least 2,000Wh of capacity and a continuous output rating of 1,800W or higher, which gives you headroom above a 1,500W heater rather than running right at the ceiling. Fast AC recharge matters more here than almost any other use case on this site, since you'll likely want to top the unit off between short bursts of heater use rather than running it down once and waiting. And if you're buying primarily for winter outage backup, weigh a lower-wattage 750-1,000W heater against the bigger, pricier station a full 1,500W unit would require. The lower-wattage combination is often the more practical setup for an apartment.
