"How long will this run my fridge?" shows up in the comments of pretty much every portable power station review, and almost nobody answers it with real numbers. So here's the actual math, and what it means for picking a size.

## The two numbers you need

A mini fridge's power draw isn't one number. It's two, and mixing them up is where most people go wrong.

Running wattage is what the fridge pulls while the compressor's on and actually cooling. Most mini fridges land somewhere between 40 and 100 watts running, with a commonly cited median around 75 watts. Smaller compact units, under 2 cubic feet, tend to sit at the low end; standard mini fridges with a small freezer compartment run closer to the top.

Starting wattage, or surge, is the brief spike when the compressor kicks on. It can hit 2 to 5 times the running wattage, commonly 280 to 400 watts, for a few seconds. This matters less for total runtime and more for whether a power station can start the fridge at all. Check that the unit's surge rating clears this number, not just its continuous output rating.

Here's the part that trips people up: a fridge's compressor doesn't run nonstop. It cycles on and off to hold temperature, typically running somewhere around 30 to 50 percent of the time depending on room temperature, how full the fridge is, and how often the door opens. That's why average daily consumption, 400 to 600 Wh per day for a typical mini fridge, comes out so much lower than what you'd get by assuming the compressor runs at full wattage around the clock.

## Doing the actual math

Take a mini fridge averaging 75W running wattage, cycling roughly 40 percent of the time. That works out to an effective average draw of about 30W continuous, which lines up with the 400 to 600 Wh/day range most sources cite.

Using 500 Wh/day as a middle-of-the-road planning number, here's roughly what different power station sizes translate to:

- A ~300 Wh power station gets you roughly 12-15 hours: a solid overnight buffer, not a full day.
- A ~500-700 Wh power station gets you close to a full day, 18-24 hours, of fridge runtime on its own.
- A ~1,000 Wh power station gets you a day and a half to two days, assuming nothing else is drawing from it at the same time.

Treat these as planning estimates, not guarantees. Your actual runtime depends on your specific fridge's efficiency, the room temperature (hot room, more cycling), how full it is (a full fridge holds cold better), and how often the door opens. If the outage risk actually matters to you, round up rather than down.

## What changes the math

A full fridge beats an empty one. Cold mass, food and drinks, holds temperature between compressor cycles, so a well-stocked fridge cycles less than a mostly empty one. If you know an outage is coming, filling empty space with water bottles genuinely helps.

Room temperature matters more than people expect. A fridge in an 85°F room during a summer outage cycles noticeably more than the same fridge at 68°F, and that alone can meaningfully shrink your runtime estimate.

Running anything else off the same unit eats into fridge runtime proportionally. Charging phones, running a router, keeping a lamp on, all of that comes out of the same battery, so subtract it before estimating fridge-only runtime.

Door discipline matters more than people think during an actual outage. Every time it opens, warm air gets in and the compressor has to run longer to recover. It's a bigger factor than it sounds like.

## Practical sizing takeaway

If the goal is just keeping the fridge cold overnight during a typical outage, 300-500 Wh is usually enough. Want a full day of fridge runtime with some margin to charge a few devices too? Plan for 500-800 Wh. Prepping for multi-day outages, or running other things off the same unit? That's where 1,000+ Wh starts to make sense.
