"How long will this run my fridge?" is the single most common question buried in portable power station reviews, and it's rarely answered with real numbers. Here's the actual math, plus what it means for picking a capacity size.

## The two numbers you need

A mini fridge's power draw isn't one number — it's two, and mixing them up is the most common mistake.

**Running wattage** is what the fridge draws while the compressor is actually on and cooling. Most mini fridges use somewhere between 40 and 100 watts while running, with a commonly cited median around 75 watts. Smaller compact units (under 2 cubic feet) tend to sit at the low end of that range; standard mini fridges with a small freezer compartment run closer to the top.

**Starting (surge) wattage** is the brief spike when the compressor kicks on, which can hit 2-5x the running wattage — commonly 280-400 watts for a few seconds. This matters less for total runtime and more for whether a power station can start the fridge at all: check that the unit's surge/peak output rating clears this number, not just its continuous output rating.

Critically, a fridge's compressor doesn't run continuously — it cycles on and off to maintain temperature, typically running somewhere around 30-50% of the time depending on ambient temperature, how full the fridge is, and how often the door opens. That's why average daily consumption (400-600 Wh per day for a typical mini fridge) is much lower than what you'd get by assuming the compressor runs 24 hours a day at full running wattage.

## Doing the actual math

Take a mini fridge averaging 75W running wattage, cycling roughly 40% of the time. That works out to an effective average draw of about 30W continuous — which lines up with the commonly cited 400-600 Wh/day range for a full day of operation.

Against that, here's roughly what different power station capacity tiers translate to, using a 500 Wh/day estimate as a middle-of-the-road planning number:

- **~300 Wh power station:** roughly 12-15 hours of mini fridge runtime — a solid overnight buffer, not a full day.
- **~500-700 Wh power station:** approximately a full day (18-24 hours) of mini fridge runtime on its own.
- **~1,000 Wh power station:** roughly a day and a half to two days of mini fridge runtime, assuming nothing else is drawing power from it simultaneously.

These are planning estimates, not guarantees — your actual runtime depends on the specific fridge's efficiency, ambient temperature (a fridge in a hot room cycles more often), how full it is (a fuller fridge holds cold better and cycles less), and how often the door opens. Treat these numbers as a starting point for sizing, then round up rather than down if the outage risk matters to you.

## What changes the math

**A full fridge outperforms an empty one.** Cold mass (food and drinks) holds temperature between compressor cycles, so a well-stocked fridge cycles less often than a mostly-empty one. If you're prepping for a known outage, filling empty space with water bottles before it hits genuinely helps.

**Ambient room temperature matters more than people expect.** A fridge in an 85°F room during a summer outage will cycle noticeably more than the same fridge in a 68°F room, which can meaningfully shrink your runtime estimate.

**Running anything else off the same power station shortens fridge runtime proportionally.** If you're also charging phones, running a router, or keeping lights on from the same unit, subtract that draw from your total before estimating fridge-only runtime.

**Door discipline matters during an actual outage.** Every door open lets warm air in and triggers a longer compressor cycle to recover. This is a bigger factor than most people assume when stretching a limited power budget.
