A few years ago, "budget power station" meant a sad little lithium-ion brick that lost a third of its capacity within a year and made you nervous about leaving it plugged in overnight. That's changed. The sub-$300 tier has actually caught up to where the expensive units were not long ago, and the battery chemistry inside them is now the same LiFePO4 cells used in units that cost four times as much. If you've been holding off because you assumed "cheap" meant "compromised," it's worth another look.

This isn't a full lab-tested ranking. It's a practical rundown of what's worth considering at this price point if you rent, live in a smaller space, and want something that covers a router, a laptop, a CPAP, or a fridge during a short outage without needing a truck to move it.

That's enough to run a phone, laptop, router, modem, and a few lights through a multi-hour outage, or to keep a CPAP going overnight. ## Why the battery chemistry matters even at this price

Every model above uses LiFePO4 cells rather than the older NMC lithium-ion chemistry that used to dominate budget power stations. That's the detail actually worth paying attention to when comparing options, more than wattage or design. LiFePO4 cells are rated for something like 3,000 to 5,000 charge cycles versus 500 to 1,000 for standard lithium-ion, and they're meaningfully more stable if something goes wrong internally. We go deeper on why that distinction matters for apartment use specifically in our [LiFePO4 vs. lithium-ion breakdown](/Logans-site/2026/07/09/lifepo4-vs-lithium-ion-portable-power-station-safety/), but the short version is: at this price, you're no longer trading safety and longevity for a lower number on the price tag. That tradeoff mostly disappeared.

## What you're giving up compared to pricier units

Don't expect solar input speeds or capacities that rival $600+ stations, and don't expect these to run anything power-hungry like a space heater, a hair dryer, or a full-size fridge compressor for more than a short stretch. Output caps in the 300-600W range rule those out. What you do get is enough runway for the devices that matter most in a short outage: phone charging, a laptop, a router and modem, some LED lighting, and a CPAP if you're not running heated tubing. For a lot of renters, that's actually the entire use case, and paying more for headroom you'll never use doesn't make sense.

If your household leans toward "keep the internet and a couple of devices alive for a few hours," a sub-$300 LiFePO4 unit does that job well and won't feel like a compromise. If you also need to keep a fridge running through a longer outage, budget for the next tier up instead of trying to stretch one of these past what it's built for.

## A few things worth checking before you buy

Pass-through charging, meaning the ability to plug the station into the wall while it's simultaneously powering your devices, isn't universal at this price. If you want to use one as a mini UPS for a router or modem so the internet never blinks during a brief outage, confirm the specific model supports it before assuming it does. Some budget units handle this fine; a few older ones don't like being charged and discharged at the same time and will shut off as a safety measure.

Outlet count matters more than people expect too. A 300Wh unit with only one AC outlet forces you to choose between the fridge and the router when both need power at once. If you know you'll be running two or three things simultaneously, check the outlet layout on the spec page rather than assuming more capacity automatically means more ports.

Warranty length is a reasonable stand-in for how the manufacturer feels about long-term reliability, and it varies more than you'd think at this price tier. Most budget stations offer somewhere between 1 and 2 years, with the Anker C300X's 5-year coverage being the outlier on this list. That's not nothing: a battery that fails in year three costs you the whole unit if there's no coverage left.

## Is a budget station enough, or should you spend more

The honest answer depends on what you're protecting against. If your outages are usually a few hours and your main worry is losing internet, light, and phone charging, a 200-300Wh LiFePO4 unit is genuinely enough, and spending more just buys headroom you won't use. If you've got a CPAP user in the house, a mini fridge with medication in it, or outages that regularly stretch past 8 hours, it's worth stepping up to the 500Wh-plus range instead.
