Every time the power goes out for more than a few hours, someone in the group chat suggests "just get a generator." For a homeowner with a garage and a driveway, that might be reasonable advice. For most renters, it's a plan that never actually works, and in a lot of buildings it's flatly against the rules.

Here's the thing nobody tells you at the hardware store: a gas generator and a portable power station solve the same problem in completely different ways, and only one of them is realistic if you live in an apartment, a condo, or a rented house without a detached garage.

## Why gas generators are usually a non-starter for renters

Most apartment buildings fall under IBC Occupancy Group R-2 or R-3 in the building code, and those classifications generally prohibit running internal combustion engines inside units, hallways, stairwells, or on covered balconies. That's not a suggestion landlords made up to annoy you. Fire codes based on the International Fire Code and NFPA standards restrict storing gasoline in multi-unit buildings and require exhaust clearances that a balcony or parking spot usually can't provide. Run a generator in a spot that doesn't meet those clearances and you're not just risking a fine. You're risking eviction, and worse, you're risking your neighbors' safety.

The danger is not hypothetical. The Consumer Product Safety Commission puts the average at around 100 carbon monoxide deaths per year tied to portable generators, with more than 900 deaths recorded between 2005 and 2017 alone. The majority happen in the colder months, and 81 percent occur in residential settings, often because someone ran a generator too close to a window, in a garage with the door cracked, or on a porch they assumed was ventilated enough. CO is odorless and invisible, and by the time you notice symptoms, it can already be too late. That risk barely changes even if you technically had the space to run one, which most renters don't.

There's also the mechanical reality that even if you had a legal spot for a generator, most rentals don't have anywhere secure to store several gallons of gasoline, which itself is a fire hazard sitting in a coat closet or under a sink.

## What a portable power station actually gives up in return

None of this means portable power stations do everything a generator can. A mid-size gas generator can put out 3,000 to 7,000 watts continuously and run for as long as you keep feeding it fuel. Most portable power stations top out well under that, and once the battery is empty, you're waiting hours to recharge from a wall outlet unless you've got solar panels to top it off during the day. If your goal is running central air conditioning or a well pump through a multi-day outage, a power station in the renter-friendly price range isn't going to get you there, and pretending otherwise sets you up for disappointment.

For what most apartment outages actually require, though, a power station is not really a compromise. Keeping a router, a laptop, some lights, a phone, and a CPAP running through a several-hour outage is well within range of even a mid-tier unit, and units in the 1000Wh-plus class can stretch that further and keep a mini fridge going too. ## The noise and hassle difference is bigger than people expect

Even setting aside legality and safety, a gas generator running outside your window at 2 a.m. is loud enough to annoy every neighbor on your floor, which is its own way to end up on a landlord's bad side. A portable power station is silent. There's no fuel to store, no oil to change, no pull cord, and no exhaust to route away from windows. You charge it, you keep it in a closet, and it's ready when you need it. For apartment living specifically, that convenience matters more than raw wattage most of the time.

Cost is closer than people assume, too. A 3,500-watt gas generator from a name brand runs somewhere in the same $400 to $700 range as a solid 1000Wh-1500Wh portable power station, before you factor in the ongoing cost of gasoline and the annual maintenance a combustion engine needs to keep starting reliably after sitting unused for months. Power stations with LiFePO4 batteries just sit there charged and ready, no maintenance required, for years.

## What about small inverter generators marketed as "quiet"

Some renters ask about the small inverter generators sold for camping and tailgating, the ones marketed as quieter and cleaner-running than a standard contractor generator. They are quieter, and the exhaust is cleaner than an old-style generator, but neither of those changes the fire code classification or the carbon monoxide risk. A 2,000-watt inverter generator still burns gasoline and still produces CO, and running one on a balcony or breezeway doesn't become legal just because the manufacturer's marketing calls it apartment-friendly. If a lease or condo bylaw prohibits combustion engines in a unit, it prohibits the quiet ones too. Check your specific lease before assuming a smaller unit gets a pass, but don't be surprised if the answer is still no.

## When a generator might still make sense

If you own a house with a garage, a detached shed, or outdoor space that meets clearance requirements, and you regularly deal with multi-day outages, a generator paired with a transfer switch is a legitimate option that a power station can't fully replace. That's a different situation from apartment living, though, and worth being honest about. The comparison in this post is specifically about rented units where storage, ventilation, and fire code all work against a combustion engine, not a blanket claim that generators are pointless.

## So which one should you actually buy

If you rent, the decision is more straightforward than it might feel. A gas generator is illegal to run in most apartment settings, dangerous even where it's technically allowed, and requires storage most rentals don't have. A portable power station with a LiFePO4 battery handles the outage scenarios that actually come up in apartment living: internet, lights, medical devices, phones, and a fridge for a stretch, without the carbon monoxide risk or the noise complaints.
